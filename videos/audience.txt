Programmers who write code every day — but have never looked underneath it.

They know what a function call does.
They don't know what the CPU does to execute it.
They've heard of registers, opcodes, and syscalls — but those words feel like black magic.
Your job is to make them feel like an insider by the end of each video.

- Junior devs
- Mid-level engineers
- Senior devs
- Knows at least one language
- Skips syntax explainers
- Curious, not academic

Before: "I just use the abstraction."
After: "Now I know what's actually happening."

Do
- Connect every concept to code they already write
- Use analogies before definitions
- End each video teasing the next layer
- Move fast — respect their time
- Show the messy reality, not just clean theory
- Celebrate the "aha" moment explicitly

Don't
- Explain what a variable or loop is
- Use academic language without grounding it
- Wrap up neatly — leave a hook open
- Pad with recaps they don't need
- Assume hardware knowledge (they're software people)
- Make seniors feel talked down to

1. Cut the pause, keep the breath
Edit out dead air but don't go robotic. The energy is a smart friend explaining something cool — not a lecture, not a podcast ramble.

2. Hook in the first 30 seconds
Start with the most surprising or counterintuitive thing in the video. "Your for-loop isn't a loop to your CPU" beats "Today we're going to talk about instruction pipelining."

3. One concept per video, fully resolved
Don't leave them confused. Leave them curious. There's a difference — confusion is unresolved, curiosity is satisfied but hungry for more.

4. The cliffhanger is the series engine
Every video ends by making the next one feel necessary. "We can now execute instructions — but how does the CPU know which instruction to run next?" That question is the next video.

Think 3Blue1Brown's clarity meets Fireship's pace. Visually driven, fast-cutting, but never sacrifices the "why" for the sake of speed.
The viewer should feel like they're being let in on a secret — not lectured at.
